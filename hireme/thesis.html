The Effect of Tangible and Multitouch Interfaces on Game Performance

A Senior Thesis Project in Human-Computer Interaction

Role: Interaction Design, Hardware, Code, Statistical Analysis

For my undergraduate thesis, I conducted original HCI research surrounding tangible and multitouch interfaces in gaming. I was trying to explore the question of how the tactility of a player's physical interaction with a game affects their emotional response.

Specifically, I focused on tangible interfaces using the iPad. I built a simple twin-stick shooter with three different input methods: a traditional "dual stick" input with virtual d-pads, a one-handed touch interface, and a tangible game piece.

I designed a simple physical game piece capable of accurately determining both the position and angle of rotation of the piece when placed on the screen. Using no electric or moving components made it cheap, sturdy, and easy to iterate on.

The game also featured optional multitouch gestures meant to be performed with one hand that made the game significantly easier. Given that two of the input methods only required one hand, I was interested in seeing how the different methods would impact users' willingness to use gestures with their secondary hand.

Process

Fifteen test participants were brought in for intensive play sessions. Each participant played the game using all three control schemes: interviews and video footage provided qualitative information about their experience, while a comprehensive in-game analytics system collected quantitative data.

I analyzed the data, focusing mainly on participants' perceived performance for each interface, their actual performance, how much fun they had, and their use of the gestures. Ultimately, fifteen participants was too small a sample size to prove anything of statistical significance from the quantitative data, but it was clear from the interviews that the more visceral the interface, the more engaged the participants felt in the game.
